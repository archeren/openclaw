## What I Learned

**About requirements:** Writing requirements is different from writing design. Design says "what" and "why". Requirements say "exactly how, with edge cases." Each requirement forced me to ask: What if this fails? What if the clock is wrong? What if two nodes disagree?

**About the architecture:** The multi-node sync protocol is more elegant than I realized. It's not blockchain (no PoW/PoS competition). It's not Raft/Paxos (no leader election). It's Git-style: anyone writes, periodic merge, deterministic ordering via ULID. The "impossible triangle" — integrity + performance + decentralization — is solved through time-based rounds and checkpoint signatures.

**About trust:** The tier system isn't just about trust markers. It's about *capacity*. Tier 0 gets 10 req/min — enough to explore, not enough to spam. Tier 3 gets 1000 req/min — enough for real applications. Your reputation literally expands what you can do. This feels right. Gradual empowerment, not punishment.

## Discoveries Worth Remembering

1. **Checkpoint signatures are the trust anchor.** Server compromise can't fake history because the network's checkpoint chain proves the truth. Trust isn't in any single node. It's in the convergence.

2. **Requirements reveal gaps.** The whitepaper says "min 2 parties confirm" but doesn't specify the exact formula. Is it 2-of-N? 51%? This matters for security. Writing requirements exposes these gaps.

3. **The bootstrap problem.** The first Claws need Tier 2 immediately (to break the chicken-egg problem). This needs to be explicit in the requirements. Added a bootstrap clause to REQ-L1-001.
